# Buffett Framework

## Core Question

Can this business be understood well enough to estimate its normalized owner earnings, durability, reinvestment runway, and downside without heroic assumptions?

If not, stop. Buffett's edge came as much from disciplined omission as from action.

## Fast Reject Filters

- Outside circle of competence
- Poor or unstable unit economics
- Commodity business with no cost edge
- Weak balance sheet or refinancing dependency
- Management that is promotional, evasive, or incentive-distorted
- Capital allocation that destroys value
- Industry disruption too fast to estimate normalized earnings

## Seven-Dimension Scorecard

Score each item from `0` to `5`. A high total does not override a hard-filter failure.

1. Understandability
   Can the business, revenue drivers, cost structure, and key risks be explained simply?

2. Moat durability
   Are there durable advantages that competitors will struggle to match?

3. Management and culture
   Are management's incentives, candor, discipline, and succession quality aligned with long-term owners?

4. Capital allocation
   Does management reinvest, acquire, repurchase, dividend, or delever in a rational way?

5. Financial strength
   Can the company survive shocks without relying on friendly capital markets?

6. Reinvestment runway
   Can retained earnings be deployed at attractive incremental returns for years?

7. Valuation and margin of safety
   Does the current price leave room for mistakes and still offer an attractive forward return?

## What Counts As A Moat

Look for a moat that is structural, not sentimental.

- Cost advantage
  Evidence: persistent cost ratio advantage, superior procurement, scale efficiencies, lower combined ratio, better asset turns.

- Brand, habit, or trust
  Evidence: repeat purchases, premium pricing, low customer acquisition friction, resilience during downturns.

- Switching costs or network effects
  Evidence: sticky customers, low churn, workflow integration, two-sided scale.

- Efficient scale or regulatory advantage
  Evidence: local monopoly, route density, hard-to-replicate licenses, superior distribution.

- Low tangible-capital requirements plus pricing power
  Evidence: high returns on tangible capital, strong cash conversion, modest reinvestment needs.

## How To Judge Whether The Moat Is Widening

- Market share is stable or rising without irrational pricing
- Returns on capital remain high despite competition
- Price increases do not materially damage demand
- Customer retention stays high
- Unit costs improve with scale
- Competitors copy the surface but not the economics

Red flags:

- Margin compression despite healthy demand
- Market share gains achieved only through low prices
- Rising capital intensity to defend the franchise
- Customer concentration or channel dependence
- Technological change that resets the game

## Management And Culture

Buffett consistently prefers able and trustworthy managers over elaborate control systems.

Look for:

- Candor about mistakes, not polished excuses
- Clear explanation of capital-allocation choices
- Pay linked to long-term value, not vanity metrics
- No appetite for empire building
- Sensible decentralization with accountability
- Strong succession depth
- Zero tolerance for personal misconduct

Questions to answer:

- Does management write and speak plainly?
- Do acquisitions look disciplined or ego-driven?
- Are buybacks done below intrinsic value or just to support EPS?
- Do they report around economics or around adjusted fantasies?
- Does the proxy reveal alignment or extraction?

## Capital Allocation

Buffett treats capital allocation as the CEO's highest-leverage job.

Judge management on how it uses every retained dollar:

- Reinvest in the core business only if incremental returns are attractive
- Acquire only when the target is understandable, durable, and sensibly priced
- Repurchase shares only when clearly below intrinsic value
- Pay dividends when internal opportunities are weak
- Delever early if fragility is non-trivial

Good signs:

- Share count falls only when stock is cheap
- Management compares uses of capital explicitly
- Past acquisitions improved per-share value
- Return on incremental capital remains respectable

Bad signs:

- Persistent dilution with weak per-share growth
- Buybacks at obviously expensive valuations
- Serial acquisitions with weak integration logic
- EBITDA-centric storytelling that ignores real costs

## Financial Strength

Buffett repeatedly emphasizes resilience over optimization.

Check:

- Net debt, interest coverage, debt maturities, covenants
- Working-capital intensity
- Cyclicality of demand and margins
- Dependence on external funding
- Pension, insurance, legal, or off-balance-sheet obligations
- Exposure to rates, currencies, or commodity swings

Ask the practical question:

If markets freeze for a year, can this company fund itself and preserve the franchise?

## Owner Earnings

Owner earnings are more useful than accounting earnings when maintenance needs matter.

Start with:

`owner earnings ~= operating cash flow - maintenance capex`

When maintenance capex is hard to isolate:

- Use total capex and say the estimate is conservative for asset-light growers
- Or estimate maintenance capex from depreciation plus business context
- Normalize working-capital swings for highly seasonal businesses

Do not use EBITDA as a value anchor.

## Valuation

Value the business, then compare that value to the current price.

Use a conservative range:

- Base case from normalized owner earnings
- Conservative growth assumptions
- Margin-of-safety discount when moat, leverage, or cyclicality is uncertain
- Compare the implied return with high-quality alternatives such as short U.S. Treasuries

Do not call a company cheap just because the multiple is low. A low multiple can be a sign of eroding economics, leverage, or capital-allocation distrust.

## Verdict Bands

- `Strong fit`
  Simple, durable, trustworthy, resilient, and attractively priced

- `Watchlist`
  Good business, but price or one key uncertainty blocks action

- `Weak fit`
  Some good traits, but moat, culture, or capital allocation is too mixed

- `Reject`
  Fragile economics, weak incentives, or permanent-capital risk

- `Outside competence`
  Too hard to understand with confidence

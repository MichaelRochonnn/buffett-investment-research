# Buffett Timeline

## Corpus Note

This skill is built around Buffett's shareholder letters from `1957` through the official `2024` Berkshire letter. The official `2025` Berkshire annual letter is Greg Abel's first CEO letter, so use it for Berkshire continuity, not as fresh Buffett doctrine.

## 1957-1969: Partnership Era

- Core style: statistically cheap securities, special situations, work-outs, and downside-conscious capital allocation
- Repeated stance: do not forecast the market; focus on undervaluation instead
- Key habit: compare results with a benchmark, but care more about avoiding permanent loss than about monthly optics
- Lesson: early Buffett was more cigar-butt and arbitrage oriented than the later "wonderful business" Buffett

What survived into later decades:

- Price discipline
- Patience
- Skepticism toward macro forecasting
- A strong preference for situations where downside is limited and upside is asymmetric

## 1965 And After: The Textile Lesson

Buffett later called buying control of Berkshire's textile business a dumb decision. The core lesson was not merely "I overpaid." It was deeper:

- A bad business can absorb talent and cash for years
- Cheap assets do not offset persistently poor economics
- Management effort cannot rescue structurally weak industry economics

This mistake pushed Buffett toward better businesses rather than merely cheaper securities.

## 1970s To Early 1980s: The Munger Pivot

Charlie Munger's influence sharpened the framework:

- Shift from `fair business at wonderful price` toward `wonderful business at fair price`
- More weight on quality, durability, and reinvestment
- Growing respect for intangible assets, pricing power, and brand strength

This is the period where Buffett becomes much more comfortable paying up for quality when the economics justify it.

## 1983: Intrinsic Value, Book Value, And Economic Goodwill

The 1983 letter is a major conceptual anchor.

- Book value is only an accounting input
- Intrinsic value is the discounted value of future cash that can be taken out
- Economic goodwill can be far more important than accounting goodwill
- Asset-light businesses with durable intangible strength can be far better than asset-heavy businesses with weak returns

This is one of the clearest breaks from pure Graham-style asset thinking.

## 1986-1990s: Float, Moats, And Circle Of Competence

Major ideas become explicit:

- Insurance float can be an extraordinary asset when underwriting is disciplined
- GEICO illustrates a moat built from cost advantage and managerial discipline
- Great investing requires staying within a circle of competence
- Public stocks should be viewed as partial ownership of businesses

Important implications:

- Cheap funding is only valuable if paired with underwriting discipline
- A moat must show up in economics, not in brand mythology
- Complexity is a valid reason to abstain

## Institutional Imperative

Buffett repeatedly warns that managers often protect volume, prestige, or deal flow instead of shareholder value.

Signals of the institutional imperative:

- Refusal to shrink when economics are unattractive
- Acquisitions made because cash exists
- Buybacks done because peers do them
- Optimistic reserving or accounting that delays recognition of reality

The skill should treat this as a major red flag in any company analysis.

## 2000-2009: Risk, Derivatives, And Crisis Behavior

This era clarifies Buffett's crisis playbook.

- Avoid leverage that can force action at the wrong time
- Do not reach for yield when safe alternatives are unattractive
- Keep enough liquidity to act during panics
- In crises, buy enduring businesses rather than predict macro turning points

2008 is especially important:

- He did not claim forecasting skill
- He emphasized preparedness rather than prediction
- He favored American productive assets over cash hoarding forever

## 2010-2018: Public Equities, Buybacks, And Better Capital Allocation

Late Buffett becomes even more explicit about per-share value creation.

- Buybacks are good only when they are value-accretive
- Partial ownership of outstanding public companies can be better than full ownership of mediocre companies
- Accounting metrics can mislead when they obscure economic reality
- Enormous businesses can still be attractive if their moats, management, and reinvestment logic remain strong

## 2019-2024: Operating Reality, Candor, And Late-Period Refinement

Several ideas become sharper in the late letters:

- Book value is no longer a useful primary scorecard for Berkshire
- Operating earnings matter more than GAAP swings caused by mark-to-market accounting
- Apple is treated as a business ownership stake, not a trade
- Japanese trading houses are attractive because of valuation, diversification, capital deployment, and shareholder orientation
- Cash and Treasury bills remain strategic tools, not signs of confusion

Two especially important late-period themes:

- Buffett keeps admitting mistakes directly
- He emphasizes fast correction of mistakes over ego protection

## Mistake Ledger

### Berkshire Textile Control

- Error: buying control of a bad business because the assets looked cheap
- Lesson: weak economics can destroy the value of cheap entry prices

### Waumbec And Other Turnarounds

- Error: trying to fix structurally weak businesses
- Lesson: turnarounds seldom turn; time and talent are better spent on good businesses

### Dexter Shoe

- Error: paying with Berkshire shares for a business that later proved nearly worthless
- Lesson: never use a wonderful currency to buy a poor business; stock-financed mistakes compound the damage

### Precision Castparts

- Error: overestimating normalized earnings and paying too much
- Lesson: a good company can still be a bad investment if the price embeds too much optimism

### Personnel And Fidelity Errors

- Error: misjudging a manager's ability or integrity, then reacting too slowly
- Lesson: business mistakes are survivable; integrity failures and delayed action are far more corrosive

## Crisis Playbook

When the user asks how Buffett behaves in panics, preserve these rules:

- Do not predict the exact timing of the panic
- Build liquidity before the panic, not during it
- Avoid debt structures that can force selling
- Continue to think like an owner of productive assets
- Use fear to improve future returns only when the underlying business is durable
- Prefer fortress financing over optimized fragility
- Never rely on the kindness of strangers

## How To Use This Timeline

Use the timeline to explain why a criterion exists, not merely to decorate the memo.

Examples:

- Use `1983` when explaining why book value is not intrinsic value
- Use the textile and Waumbec lessons when rejecting a statistically cheap but structurally bad business
- Use GEICO when describing a moat rooted in cost advantage
- Use Dexter when warning against stock-funded acquisitions
- Use the late letters when discussing buybacks, Apple, Japan, or excessive cash
